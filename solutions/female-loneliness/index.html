You're supposed to have a tribe. A squad. A group chat that never sleeps. The reality: 16% of women feel lonely or isolated all the time, and the emotional labor of maintaining everyone else's wellbeing leaves nothing for your own.

Female loneliness is invisible because women are expected to be social. Society assumes women have friend groups, support systems, emotional intelligence. Pew Research 2025 shows women report MORE lack of deep emotional support despite having wider networks. Width isn't depth.

The emotional labor is crushing. Women are the ones who plan the dinners, remember the birthdays, check in on friends, hold space for everyone's feelings. But who holds space for you? The person everyone turns to often has nobody to turn to herself.

And the competition is real. Female friendships can carry an undercurrent of comparison that male friendships don't: careers, relationships, bodies, parenting choices. Social media amplifies it. The friendships that look perfect online might be hollow underneath.

Women are lonely too but nobody talks about it because we're supposed to have big friend groups.

What this actually does to you.

The Emotional Labor Drain

You support everyone and nobody supports you

You're the one who remembers, who checks in, who plans, who listens. The emotional labor of maintaining everyone else's wellbeing leaves you depleted. When you finally need support, there's no one available because you never asked.
The Comparison Trap

Every other woman seems to have it figured out

Instagram shows friend groups at brunch, girls' trips, matching Halloween costumes. The comparison creates shame: why don't I have that? The truth is many of those women are performing connection, not experiencing it.
The Life Stage Divide

Your friends are on different timelines

She had a baby. She's traveling the world. She got married. She's building a startup. Every life milestone creates distance. The friend group that bonded in college fragments as everyone's life diverges. Nobody's wrong. Everyone's lonely.

What actually works.

Specific strategies backed by research and real experience.

01

Create a women's circle.

4-6 women. Monthly. 10 minutes each of check-in without advice, just listening. The structure prevents it from becoming another obligation. Find circles near you.

02

Stop being the planner for everyone.

If you're always organizing, you're always giving. Let someone else plan. If nobody does, that tells you something about the reciprocity in your friendships.

03

Practice 'reverse people-pleasing.'

Refuse one social obligation per week. Use that time for a friendship YOU chose, not one you owe. Break the pattern.

04

Find friends outside your life stage.

Your best friend doesn't need to be at the same life milestone. Some of the deepest friendships cross the stage divide. A child-free woman and a new mom can bond over shared values, not shared schedules.

05

Ask for help out loud.

The sentence that changes everything: 'I'm having a hard time and I need to talk.' Women are socialized to give, not ask. Max is here if you need a safe place to start.

06

Audit your friendships for depth.

Quality over quantity. Which 3 friends would you call at 2am? Invest there. Let the rest be what they are.
