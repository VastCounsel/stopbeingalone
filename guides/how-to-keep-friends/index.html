The number of Americans with zero close friends has quadrupled since 1990. It's not because people are worse at friendship. It's because adult life is structurally hostile to maintaining them. Work, commuting, family, errands. By the time you have free time, you're too exhausted to text anyone back. Research confirms: consistent low-effort contact predicts stronger friendships than rare big catch-ups.

The drift is invisible until it's irreversible. You don't notice the gap widening. One skipped plan becomes two. A monthly hangout becomes quarterly. Then you realize it's been 8 months since you talked to someone you used to see weekly. The ambiguity of the loss makes it harder to address.

And nobody teaches friendship maintenance. We teach romantic relationship skills, parenting skills, career skills. But friendship? You're on your own. The result is a generation of adults who know how to get promoted but not how to keep a friend.

How to deal with friends drifting apart and the loneliness that comes with it as an adult?

r/Advice

What this actually does to you.

The Slow Fade

Nobody says goodbye. They just stop showing up.

There's no fight, no breakup, no dramatic ending. Just gradually longer gaps between texts, plans that never materialize, and the slow realization that someone who used to matter is now a stranger you follow on Instagram.
The Logistics Wall

Scheduling a hangout feels like planning a summit

Between two adults' work schedules, family obligations, and exhaustion, finding a mutually available 2-hour window takes 3 weeks of back-and-forth texting. By then, the momentum is dead and nobody follows through.
The One-Sided Effort

You're always the one reaching out

You text first. You suggest plans. You follow up. And when you stop, silence. The asymmetry feels personal but it's usually just adult life. Most people are drowning in their own logistics and assume the friendship is fine.

What actually works.

Not platitudes. Specific strategies backed by research.

01

Create a friendship tracker.

A simple note with each close friend, last contact date, and next planned interaction. When you see '3 months since contact,' that's your prompt. Max can help you stay accountable.

02

Set quarterly recurring anchors.

First Saturday of every quarter: walk and coffee with [friend]. Lock it in the calendar. Recurring events survive logistics better than 'let's hang out sometime.'

03

Use batching.

Group 2-4 friends into one recurring low-effort event: monthly game night, group text thread, quarterly dinner. One event maintains multiple friendships.

04

Lower the bar for contact.

A 30-second voice note counts. A meme with 'this is you' counts. A text that says 'thinking of you' counts. Friendship maintenance doesn't require dinner dates.

05

Be the organizer without resentment.

Someone has to be the planner. Accept that role without keeping score. The alternative to being the one who reaches out is having nobody reach out at all.

06

Have the honest conversation.

If a friendship is fading and you want to save it, say so: 'Hey, I feel like we've been drifting. I miss you. Can we lock in a regular thing?' Most people are relieved someone finally named it.
