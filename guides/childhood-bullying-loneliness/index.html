Childhood bullying doesn't just hurt in the moment. It rewires your social brain. Research shows bullying victims have 2-3x higher adult loneliness. The rejection sensitivity it creates turns every social situation into a potential threat.

You learned that people aren't safe. That being visible means being targeted. That the safest option is invisibility. These lessons were wrong, but your nervous system still believes them 20 years later.

The self-isolation feels like protection but it's a prison. You avoid groups, deflect attention, keep conversations shallow. The anxiety patterns that started in a school hallway now control your adult social life.

The bullying never really stops inside my head even 20 years later.

r/CPTSD

What this actually does to you.

The Trust Wound

You assume people will hurt you

Every new person is a potential bully until proven otherwise. The hypervigilance exhausts you before the conversation even starts. Trust was broken so early that rebuilding it feels impossible.
The Invisibility Strategy

You hide to survive

You sit in the back. You don't raise your hand. You deflect compliments. The strategy that kept you safe as a kid now keeps you isolated as an adult. Safety and loneliness became the same thing.
The Inner Critic

The bully lives inside your head now

The kids who mocked you are long gone. But their voice became your internal monologue. 'You're weird.' 'Nobody likes you.' 'Don't even try.' The external bullying became internal self-sabotage.

What actually works.

Specific strategies backed by research.

01

Try graduated rejection exposure therapy.

Work with a therapist specializing in trauma. Start with low-stakes social situations and build evidence of safety. Each positive interaction weakens the old wiring.

02

Join an adult survivors of bullying group.

Online groups with structured sharing. Hearing others' stories normalizes yours and breaks the isolation of 'nobody understands.'

03

Practice the inner child letter.

Write weekly letters from your adult self to the child who was bullied. Read them before social situations. Max can help you process what comes up.

04

Keep an evidence log of safety.

After every interaction, note one sign that the person was safe: they smiled, they listened, they responded warmly. Build counter-evidence against the old programming.

05

Separate past from present.

When anxiety spikes in social situations, name it: 'This is my bullying response, not reality.' The distinction creates space between the trigger and the reaction.

06

Invest in one safe friendship.

You don't need to trust everyone. Find one person who feels safe and invest there. Depth with one person heals more than breadth with many.
